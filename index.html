Visceria

Developed by newly-founded and up-and-coming development studio Gaming Thing Studios, Visceria has you playing as a stranded alien girl named Melody, when her ship crashes on a strange, unknown planet. Looking to repair her ship after the crash, she sets off to find parts and tools to help; along the way, she encounters many friend and powerful foe.


As Melody wonders around the semi-populated planet, she stumbles upon books detailing the seven ancient monarchs, the embodiment of wisdom, of power, "the seven true gods", they say. These books too describe the tale of a young child who lost their way, and their family; the child decided to venture out in search of the seven monarchs' wisdom.

Story

An overview of the story and lore of Visceria, including the official book entries.

Melody awakens after crash-landing her ship on an unknown planet. Panicked, she thinks to search the area for parts and tool to repair her vessel, and hopefully, return to her original route. Along the way she finds many interesting creatures, some friend, some foe. Melody also finds a few books, detaling the old age of seven ancient monarchs. These monarchs ruled as gods, gods of wisdom and power, none to cross them.

Entry #1: Departure

Now, I will tell you a story...


A long, long time ago, in a land far distant from other life, there was a kingdom, ruled by seven monarchs. These monarchs were regarded as the embodiment of wisdom and power, and were treated like gods by many. These monarchs divided themselves into the ranks their varying strengths demanded: the Lower Trinity, the Upper Trinity, and the High Liege. The Lower Trinity, the weakest of the seven, were designated to deal with the more trivial matters of their realm; the Upper Trinity, those stronger than the former, were designated as their realms' ruler; and the High Liege, strongest among the monarchs, ruled over all the kingdom's realms and the other monarchs. The Lower and Upper Trinities spread themselves with a pair for every realm. They ruled with leisure for many years, with only occasional squabbles among themselves. But these monarchs are not who our story follows.


One day, a child in this kingdom—starving, dirty, and on death's door—stumbled from their family's home. The family had suffered a sudden accident, and the child awoke to find themselves alone in the cellar. Where most children would sob and rot in the home, waiting for someone to save them, this child was no fool; in fact, they happened to be one of above-average intelligence. Knowing that tears would not help them (for no crying had ever affected their parents' decisions anyway) they left their home to seek the wisdom of the kingdom's monarchs, thinking that if anyone would know what to do, it would be them.

Entry #2: Puryd of the Lower Trinity

Eventually, after walking many days, the child had entered one of the kingdom's realms. It was a dazzling place, with bright lights contrasted on the dark outlines of enormous buildings, bigger than the child had ever dreamed of. Following their instincts, the child entered into the underground tunnels of the realm's waste. The dank corridors were adorned in mold and stains, turning the yellow bricks almost black. The smell tore at their nostrils, a sickeningly-sweet mix of feces and neglect--but the child was not alone in these sewers. Finally, the child encountered them.


His eyes drew the attention of anyone, with a constant look of self-righteousness and glee; his grin, too, gave him the look of someone forever in ecstasy. Between these traits and the overall put-togetherness of his look, the individual dripped with confidence. The eyes, the child noticed, didn't just seem to radiate fulfillment, though. Peculiarly, they seemed to always be squinted in the way one might while looking at a bright light. Equally odd and noticeable were the individual's two advisors, who never left their master's side. Their names were Hugo and Vanty, but the child would never know this, for the pair never did anything but whisper compliments and approval in the ears of their master. Lastly was the mirror. The individual always had one of his advisors holding a mirror he would sneak glances at every couple of seconds, as though bored with reality.


This was the realm's Lower, Puryd.
The child conversed with Puryd for a spell, but Puryd had no knowledge of the child's situation, nor how to fix it. He merely gave the location of the closest monarch the child could seek. Before the child could make off, Puryd gave the child a morsel of his wisdom.
“Listen well, child—joy is reaped only on the pedestal you stand on, and good harvests come to those closest to the sun.”
Then the child, lesson in hand, was gone, in search of the next monarch.

Entry #3: Abel of the Upper Trinity

The child walked on, leaving Puryd behind to make for the imposing smokestacks which populated the territory of the next monarch. Smog filled the air, tearing at the child's lungs and blinding them as they entered the imposing entrance to the castle-like structure of gleaming metal and fire. Pipes lined the walls, steam hissing from loose valves as workers dashed through hallways and across catwalks doing maintenance checks. Sweat beaded the foreheads of everyone there, heat radiating from unseen furnaces to power the vast machine-like structure. At the top level of the structure, where normally an attic would be, the child was faced by the monarch.


Her skin was mottled and gray, a color hard to make out due to the burned red flesh. Her presence radiated hate and loathing more piercing than the blaze of the fires belching smoke through the chimneys. Indeed, from her loosely-stitched mouth, there came an amber glow, ashes floating lazily into the air whenever it was left ajar. Fiddling tentatively with the wire of the stitching, her spindly “fingers” clearly longed to rip the restraints off; interestingly, her fingers were in-fact long, delicate blades that led to a simple mechanism in the place of where her left arm would've been. One needed only to see the dark stains on these blades to know that their wielder was more than capable of dealing with those ignorant enough to accidentally wander the grounds of her territory.


This was the realm's Upper, Abel.
Once again, the child discussed their situation with the monarch, and yet again they had no wisdom to remedy the issue. Just as the child prepared to make haste for the next monarch, Abel halted them with a parting wisdom.
“Never forget, truth is power, and hate is truth—to hate something is to see without the blindfold others impose on us.”
Then the child, lesson in hand, was gone, in search of the next monarch.
